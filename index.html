SCENE 1 — POWER vs PERFORMANCE

The trade-off every processor faces

Why can't processors always run at maximum speed?

Pdynamic = C × V² × f
C = capacitance  ·  V = voltage  ·  f = clock frequency
High Performance Mode
Clock frequency: 3.0–5.0 GHz
Supply voltage: 1.0–1.2 V
TDP (Thermal Design Power): 45–125 W
Heavy workloads: gaming, rendering
Battery drain: fast (2–4 hrs)
Power-Saving Mode
Clock frequency: 0.3–1.0 GHz
Supply voltage: 0.7–0.9 V
TDP: 1–5 W
Light workloads: browsing, calls
Battery life: extended (10–20 hrs)

Power consumption grows as square of voltage — doubling V quadruples power draw

SCENE 5 — SUMMARY & KEY LEARNING OUTCOMES

What we learned

Three core techniques that define modern energy-efficient processor design

01
Power vs Performance Trade-off
Dynamic power P = CV²f shows that power grows quadratically with voltage. Every processor must balance performance demand against thermal and power budgets.
02
Dynamic Voltage & Frequency Scaling
DVFS allows the OS to reduce clock speed and supply voltage during light workloads, significantly cutting power without noticeable performance loss.
03
ARM big.LITTLE Architecture
Pairing high-performance "big" cores with power-efficient "LITTLE" cores on one die allows the HMP scheduler to route tasks optimally for energy efficiency.
P = C × V² × f (dynamic power)
DVFS: lower V → P ∝ V²
HMP: right task → right core
TDP = thermal design power limit
Conclusion: Modern SoCs like the Snapdragon 8 Gen 3 combine all three techniques — DVFS on each core cluster, big.LITTLE heterogeneous processing, and intelligent OS scheduling — to deliver desktop-class performance in a 5W power envelope. This is why today's smartphones can last 20+ hours while also running high-fidelity games.
